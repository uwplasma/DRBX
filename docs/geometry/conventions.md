# Geometry conventions (1D, 2D, 3D)

`jaxdrb` supports **three** closely related “geometries”, which correspond to three solver regimes:

1. **1D flux-tube / field-line models** (linear and conservative nonlinear gates)
2. **2D perpendicular-box nonlinear testbeds** (HW2D, DRB2D)
3. **3D FCI plane stacks** (DRB3D milestone operators with open-field-line targets/plates)

This page fixes the coordinate conventions used throughout the code and documentation, and
explains what “sheath/target boundary conditions” mean in each context.

![Geometry conventions schematic](../assets/images/geometry_overview.png)

## 1D flux-tube / field-line (linear solvers)

In the **field-line** formulation, we evolve profiles along a parallel coordinate $\ell$,
while representing perpendicular dependence by Fourier modes:

$$
\tilde f(\psi,\alpha,\ell,t) = \hat f(\ell,t)\,\exp\{ i k_x \psi + i k_y \alpha \}.
$$

Here:

- $\ell$ is distance-like along the magnetic field (the “parallel” coordinate),
- $\psi$ is a *radial-like* Clebsch label,
- $\alpha$ is a *binormal-like* Clebsch label,
- $(k_x, k_y)$ are the perpendicular wavenumbers.

Perpendicular operators become algebraic in $(k_x,k_y)$ through a geometry-provided metric:

$$
k_\perp^2(\ell)=k_x^2\,g^{xx}(\ell) + 2k_xk_y\,g^{xy}(\ell) + k_y^2\,g^{yy}(\ell).
$$

This is the geometry used by the CLI workflows such as `jaxdrb-scan` and by the matrix-free
linearization (`J·v`) used by the Arnoldi solver.

**Sheath/MPSE in 1D.** When the field line is **open**, the endpoints of the $\ell$ grid are
interpreted as *magnetic-pre-sheath entrance* (MPSE) nodes, where physics closures such as
Bohm entrance, Loizu-style constraints, and optional heat/SEE knobs can be applied.
See `model/boundary-conditions.md` and `model/extensions.md`.

## 2D perpendicular boxes (nonlinear testbeds)

The nonlinear 2D models (HW2D, DRB2D) are defined on a rectangular **perpendicular** domain
$(x,y)$.

The intent is to validate conservative operators and solver choices in a fast setting.
Unless explicitly stated otherwise:

- the magnetic field is taken to be **out of the plane** (a slab guide field),
- $x$ is “radial-like” and $y$ is “binormal/poloidal-like”,
- the default numerical boundary condition is **periodic in both $x$ and $y$**.

In this 2D setting there is **no explicit parallel direction**, and thus no physical sheath
closure: “sheath” is a parallel/end-plate concept and is treated in the 1D and 3D-FCI branches.

**Why still call it edge/SOL relevant?** Many discrete pieces that matter in SOL turbulence
codes are already present and testable in 2D:

- conservative $E\times B$ advection kernels (Arakawa Jacobian),
- Poisson/polarization inversions,
- curvature/interchange-like drive operators,
- dissipation/hyperdiffusion controls,
- regression gates on long-time behavior (e.g. “zonal collapse” metrics).

These are prerequisites for production 3D simulations.

## 3D FCI plane stacks (open-field-line targets/plates)

In the FCI approach, fields are represented on a stack of planes:

- plane index: $k$ (often a toroidal angle $\varphi_k$),
- in-plane coordinates: $(x,y)$ (for ESSOS toroidal planes, these are typically $(R,Z)$),
- the parallel direction is **constructed** by tracing field lines from plane $k$ to planes $k\pm1$.

An FCI map encodes, for each plane-$k$ grid point:

- interpolation indices/weights to evaluate $f_{k\pm1}$ at the traced footpoints,
- $\Delta \ell$ (distance along the field line between planes),
- open-field-line metadata: whether the segment hits a **plate/target** before reaching the next plane.

The discrete parallel derivative is then:

$$
\partial_\parallel f\big|_k \approx
\frac{f_{k+1}(\text{foot}^+) - f_{k-1}(\text{foot}^-)}{2\,\Delta \ell},
$$

in the interior, with one-sided/non-uniform stencils near targets when the map carries hit
distances (`dl_hit`) and masks (`hit`).

**Sheath/target BCs in 3D.** In the 3D FCI branch, “sheath” means *end-plates along the magnetic field*:
field-line segments can intersect a material boundary and the model applies plate/sheath closures
as localized sink/source terms with explicit particle/energy budgets.

The implementation is described in `fci/index.md`, `fci/maps.md`, and in the DRB3D examples in
`examples/09_fci/`.

## Boundary conditions summary

| Branch | Coordinates | Default BCs | “Sheath” meaning |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1D flux-tube | $(\psi,\alpha,\ell)$ with Fourier in $(\psi,\alpha)$ | periodic or open in $\ell$ (configurable) | MPSE endpoints on open field lines |
| 2D testbeds | $(x,y)$ | periodic in $x,y$ | not applicable (no parallel direction) |
| 3D FCI | planes $k$ with in-plane $(x,y)$ | periodic plane stack or open with target hits | plate/target intersections along traced field lines |
